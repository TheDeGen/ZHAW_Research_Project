# Data Preprocessing

The data preprocessing pipeline comprised two parallel workflows—one for news data and one for energy market data—which were subsequently integrated to construct the final analytical dataset. This section details the systematic procedures employed to ensure data quality, relevance, and temporal alignment across heterogeneous sources, with particular emphasis on the iterative refinement process designed to isolate energy-relevant news content.

## News Corpus Deduplication

Following data collection, the raw news corpora underwent a rigorous quality assurance and filtering pipeline to ensure topical relevance whilst eliminating redundant and off-topic material. Initial deduplication was performed on both corpora based on exact title matching, a preprocessing step that has been demonstrated to improve downstream model performance and reduce training inefficiencies (Lee et al., 2021). This procedure reduced the English corpus from 105,725 raw articles to 89,517 unique articles (16,208 duplicates removed) and the German corpus from 133,444 raw articles to 122,208 unique articles (11,236 duplicates removed). Whilst the broad keyword-based retrieval strategy ensured comprehensive coverage of potentially relevant content, it inevitably introduced substantial quantities of off-topic material, necessitating a rigorous filtering process.
To address the challenge of off-topic content, an iterative refinement procedure was implemented using BERTopic (Grootendorst, 2022), an unsupervised topic modelling framework that leverages transformer-based embeddings and hierarchical clustering to identify latent thematic structures within text corpora. The BERTopic methodology operated through a systematic cycle: first, topic models were fitted to the deduplicated corpus to discover latent thematic clusters; second, human reviewers manually inspected the resulting topic labels and representative documents to identify non-energy-related themes; third, characteristic keywords and phrases associated with off-topic clusters were extracted to construct exclusion filters; fourth, articles whose titles or descriptions contained these keywords were removed from the corpus; finally, the updated corpus was resubmitted to BERTopic for subsequent rounds of refinement.
For the English corpus, nine iterative rounds of filtering were conducted, systematically identifying and removing articles associated with irrelevant topic categories such as sports, entertainment, crime, retail, health, weather, and general politics. Exclusion criteria were operationalised through keyword presence detection in both article titles and descriptions, ensuring that only articles whose metadata explicitly referenced excluded topics were removed. This iterative process reduced the English corpus from 89,517 deduplicated articles to 39,744 filtered articles, representing a 66% reduction. The German corpus underwent three iterative rounds targeting crime, education, sports, and tourism topics, reducing the corpus from 122,208 deduplicated articles to 113,894 filtered articles—a more modest 15% reduction, suggesting that German-language sources exhibited greater topical specificity for energy-related queries.
Following topic-based filtering, the English articles were translated into German using the Google Translate API via the deep-translator library, processed in 10 batches to optimise computational efficiency and error handling. The translated English corpus was subsequently merged with the filtered German corpus, yielding a final multilingual dataset of approximately 149,500 German-language news articles. This unified language approach facilitated consistent downstream natural language processing, as all subsequent text analyses—including zero-shot classification and sentence embedding generation—were conducted on German-language content exclusively. The decision to standardise on a single target language reflects established practices in multilingual NLP pipelines, where linguistic homogeneity simplifies model selection and reduces confounding variation attributable to cross-lingual embedding differences.
Parallel to the news data workflow, energy market data retrieved from Energy Charts (Fraunhofer ISE, n.d.) underwent a systematic preprocessing pipeline to ensure temporal alignment, feature consistency, and target variable construction. The raw data, initially stored in JSON format with 15-minute temporal granularity, were converted to CSV and resampled to hourly frequency to align with the forecasting horizon and reduce computational overhead. Key variables included spot market prices, day-ahead auction prices, generation volumes by source (wind, solar, nuclear, fossil), demand forecasts, and cross-border transmission flows.
To construct the target variable for classification, the spread between spot and day-ahead auction prices was computed for each hourly observation, and a three-class categorical target was derived using a deadband threshold of 7.0 EUR/MWh: observations with spreads exceeding +7.0 EUR/MWh were labelled 'Long', those below −7.0 EUR/MWh were labelled 'Short', and intermediate observations were classified as 'Neutral'. This deadband approach was adopted to mitigate the impact of market noise and to focus predictive modelling efforts on economically meaningful price divergences, consistent with classification frameworks employed in energy market forecasting research (Lago et al., 2020). Temporal feature engineering was performed to capture cyclical patterns and autocorrelation structures inherent in energy markets, including lagged features at 24-hour and 168-hour intervals, as well as calendar-based features such as hour of day, day of week, and month of year (Haag & Hopf, 2022). Missing values were identified in 192 hourly records (2.2% of observations) and excluded from the dataset to preserve data integrity. The final energy dataset comprised 8,734 hourly observations, each associated with 13 baseline features derived from market fundamentals and temporal attributes.
Temporal alignment between the news and energy datasets was achieved through timestamp indexing, with each hourly energy market observation linked to all news articles published within a predefined lookback window preceding the target hour. This rolling window approach enabled the aggregation of news signals into time-decay-weighted features, reflecting the hypothesis that more recent news exerts a stronger influence on market expectations than older articles (Bank for International Settlements, 2023; Shapiro et al., 2022). The application of exponential decay weighting to news features has been shown to improve forecasting accuracy by appropriately discounting stale information whilst preserving the predictive signal from recent events (Wu et al., 2025).
To preserve the temporal ordering essential for time-series forecasting, the integrated dataset was partitioned chronologically into training, validation, and test subsets with proportions of 70%, 20%, and 10%, respectively. This strict temporal split ensured that no future information could leak into model training, thereby maintaining the integrity of out-of-sample performance estimates—a critical consideration in time-series machine learning where data leakage can lead to substantially inflated performance metrics (Cerqueira, 2022). Notably, no shuffling of observations was performed at any stage of the pipeline, in accordance with best practices for time-series machine learning. The resulting preprocessed dataset, comprising 8,734 hourly observations with 13 baseline features and linked news content, served as the foundation for subsequent natural language processing and feature engineering procedures, which are detailed in the following section.
